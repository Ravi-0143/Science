# Atlas Quality Bar

**Version:** 1.0

---

## An Experience Succeeds If...

* The user lingers after completing the narrative just to experiment with the system.
* A complex, multi-variable concept can be understood without reading long text blocks.
* The user can accurately predict how the system will behave in a novel, untested state.
* The experience instills a lasting feeling of awe toward the natural world.
* A user can explain the phenomenon accurately to someone else using their own words.

---

## An Experience Fails If...

* The user feels like they are operating a standard form, spreadsheet, or video player.
* An interaction exists solely for visual flair without teaching an underlying concept.
* The user gets stuck asking *"What am I supposed to do next?"*
* Simulated physics contradicts true scientific consensus without explicit pedagogical context.
* The experience relies on artificial gamification (badges, countdown timers, points) to sustain focus.

---

## Failure and Success — The Contract View

**It has failed if:**
- The learner only scrolls.
- They never predict or experiment.
- They never discover anything themselves.
- The animation only decorates; the interaction never changes understanding.
- They forget it within five minutes.

**It has succeeded if the learner:**
- Stopped scrolling.
- Got curious.
- Started clicking.
- Started experimenting.
- Understood naturally, unprompted.
- Wants another experience.

---

## Review Checklist

Every Atlas Experience must pass these questions prior to publication:

* [ ] **Curiosity Test:** Does the arrival sequence ignite immediate curiosity before asking for cognitive effort?
* [ ] **Agency Test:** Is the user actively manipulating the core phenomenon, or merely watching a glorified animation?
* [ ] **Discovery Test:** Does the learner discover the core insight independently before the text names or defines it?
* [ ] **Scientific Truth Test:** Are the underlying physics and mathematical models demonstrably accurate?
* [ ] **Clarity of Motion Test:** Is every animation and visual transition serving a pedagogical purpose?
* [ ] **Failure Safety Test:** Is failure framed as an informative physical outcome rather than a punitive error?
* [ ] **Universal Design Test:** Is the core insight accessible to users with varying sensory, physical, or cognitive needs?
* [ ] **Atlas Identity Test:** Does this experience feel like it natively shares a universe with every other Atlas experience?

---

## Self-Audit — Before Calling Anything Done

- [ ] Do the first ten seconds create curiosity, with zero definitions?
- [ ] Is there at least one genuine "what happens if" moment?
- [ ] Could the learner discover the core idea without being told it in text?
- [ ] Does every scene hold exactly one idea?
- [ ] Would this screen look at home on an Apple product page?
- [ ] Does every interaction have a crafted, physical-feeling response?
- [ ] Does it end by pointing somewhere else, instead of just stopping?

Any "no" means it isn't ready.

---

## Golden Rules

1. **Never explain before allowing exploration.**
2. **Never animate without educational purpose.**
3. **Never break physical or mathematical truth for dramatic effect without clear disclosure.**
4. **Never treat failure as an error—treat it as data.**
5. **Never demand attention; command it through wonder.**
6. **Never lock discovery behind walls of text.**
7. **Always reward curiosity immediately.**
8. **Always make cause and effect visually or spatially obvious.**
9. **Always respect the user's cognitive bandwidth.**
10. **Always connect isolated facts to broader scientific systems.**
11. **Always design for multi-sensory understanding.**
12. **Always ensure the user feels like the primary discoverer, not a passive spectator.**
13. **Always leave the learner eager to explore the next frontier of science.**
